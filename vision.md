# Waypoint — The Vision

*Chief Visionary Draft · Feb 2026*

---

## The North Star

Most productivity tools make you feel organized. Waypoint should make you feel *powerful*.

There's a gap between having a plan and executing it. Between knowing what needs to happen and actually doing it. Between ending the day "busy" and ending the day *done*. Every tool in your stack — Slack, Notion, CRM, email — generates noise. None of them help you cut through it, sit down, and do the thing that matters.

Waypoint closes that gap.

The bet: AI doesn't just help you *manage* work. It sits beside you while you *do* the work. It learns how you think. It knows your patterns. It remembers what you drafted last week and what landed versus what didn't. Over time, it becomes the most useful collaborator you've ever had — because it never forgets, never judges, and is always ready.

The end state: You open Waypoint like you open a terminal. Everything you need is there. You know exactly what to do and why. Claude is in the room with you. You close the day with proof that it mattered.

---

## What You've Built (And Why It's Different)

The foundation is solid and the instincts are right:
- **Projects → Outcomes → Actions** is the right hierarchy. Most tools get lost in tasks. You're working at the level of *results*.
- **The Slack → inbox → triage pipeline** is the right input model. You don't generate your to-do list manually — the world sends it to you, and you decide what it means.
- **Focus Mode** with Claude as a co-pilot is the right execution model. The terminal aesthetic, the motivational quote, the command line — this is a *room*, not a button.
- **The archive ritual** is the right close. Closing the loop matters. Most tools just let things die.

Phase 2.x as scoped (Focus Mode → Context Memory → AI Breakdown → Smart Triage → Persistent Memory → Delight) is the right order. Build these in sequence. Don't skip ahead.

What follows is what comes *after* — and a few things hiding in the gaps of what you've already planned.

## The Three Gaps No One Has Filled

### Gap 1: The Tools Know What You Need To Do. None Of Them Know *When*.

You have outcomes. You have time estimates. But Waypoint doesn't know you have three meetings today, a hard stop at 4pm, or that your energy craters after lunch. There's a massive chasm between "here are your outcomes" and "here's what you can actually accomplish today given your real constraints."

The result: you stare at 7 outcomes, can't do them all, and either thrash or shut down.

**The fix:** Waypoint needs a time layer. Not a calendar app — it reads your calendar, it doesn't replace it. Based on your available windows, your energy type tags, and your known work patterns, Claude builds you a *committed day*. "You have 4 hours of real work time today. I'm suggesting these 2 outcomes and 3 actions. Here's why." You adjust, confirm, and now you have a contract with yourself. Not a wishlist — a plan.

The committed day lives inside the app as a **Today view** — the surface you reach after triage, before you start working. It's the moment of commitment. The Slack morning brief (see Gap 3) is the pre-game: it orients you before you sit down, but it's deliberately un-actionable. Decisions happen in the Today view. The brief makes you want to open the app.

### Gap 2: It Knows What You Did. It Doesn't Know If It Worked.

Every archived outcome currently has: title, actions completed, time spent, a reflection. What it doesn't have: *did it work?* Did the email campaign book meetings? Did the pitch deck get approved? Did the 5 meetings you tried to set actually materialize?

Outcomes aren't closed when actions are done. They're closed when *results land*. Right now Waypoint treats completion as the finish line. But for someone in sales, in growth, in any output-driven role — completion without outcome data is just activity tracking with extra steps.

**The fix:** Tie outcomes to results. Lightweight at first — a "result" field on archive ("booked 3 meetings," "deck approved," "campaign got 2% CTR"). Over time, connect to real data sources where they exist. But even the lightweight version changes everything: Claude can learn that your email campaigns consistently underperform on conversions even when clicks are high, and proactively suggest that the next iteration go harder on the CTA.

### Gap 3: You Have To Go To Waypoint. Waypoint Should Come To You.

The current model is pull-based. You open the app, you engage, you get value. But your best work happens when the system is *ambient* — when it catches you before you waste 20 minutes in your inbox, before you spiral on a task that isn't important, before you forget the window that's closing.

**The fix:** Waypoint pushes. A morning briefing arrives before you open the app. A midday pulse checks your trajectory. An evening wrap celebrates the wins and resets tomorrow. This isn't notification spam — it's the operating system layer that makes sure the day doesn't just *happen to you*.

One constraint matters here: the morning brief must be deliberately short and un-actionable. If you can plan your whole day from it, you don't open the app. The brief's job is to prime you and create a pull toward Waypoint — not to replace the planning moment that happens inside it.

## The Future Day in the Life

*This is 18-24 months from now. Every capability described here is buildable on the stack you have.*

**7:45am — The Morning Brief arrives in Slack (or email, your choice).**

> *Good morning. You have 3 outcomes active, 2 with deadlines this week. Your calendar shows 4.5 hours of open work time today — 2 deep blocks (9-11am, 2-4pm) and a light window (12-1pm). Based on your energy patterns, I'm suggesting: deep work on "Pitch Deck — Rohter" this morning, light prospecting tasks after lunch. New items in your inbox: 4. See you at 8:30.*

You read this on your phone before you get out of bed. You already know the shape of your day.

---

**8:30am — You open Waypoint. Left sidebar says "4 new items."**

You click. A stack of Slack messages appears. You dismiss 2, mark 2 as Add. You hit "Triage Selected →". Claude reads both together and sees they're related — one is Scott asking about the pitch deck timeline, one is a Rohter meeting scheduled for Thursday. They become: one outcome ("Pitch Deck done + Scott approved → Rohter by Thursday"), one action ("Draft v1 — 90 min, deep"), one action ("Send to Scott for review — 10 min, light").

Claude also notices: *"This pitch deck is due Thursday. Based on your history with design-heavy work, you typically need a day for revisions. That means v1 needs to be done by Tuesday. Today is Friday — you have a tight window. Want me to move it to Priority?"*

You say yes. Inbox cleared in 90 seconds.

---

**8:50am — The Today view loads.**

After triage, Waypoint doesn't dump you into all your outcomes. It pauses. Claude has read your calendar (3 meetings today, hard stop at 4pm, two real deep blocks) and your active outcomes (5 total). It proposes a committed day:

> *You have 4.5 hours of real work time today — a deep block 9–11am and another 2–4pm. I'm suggesting: "Pitch Deck — Rohter" this morning (90 min deep) and "Get 5 meetings set" this afternoon (2 light actions, ~60 min). That leaves buffer. Confirm this plan?*

You swap one thing, hit confirm. Now you have a contract — not a list of hopes, a plan with a spine. The rest of the outcomes are still there if you get ahead. But you know the shape of the day before you start.

---

**9:05am — You click Focus Mode on "Draft v1 — 90 min."**

Screen goes dark. JetBrains Mono. A quote. A blinking cursor. The timer starts.

The command line says: *"Ready when you are. Last time we worked on a pitch deck together was for the Henderson account — that one opened with market size, then problem, then your differentiated solution. Want to start there or go a different direction?"*

You didn't remember that. Claude did. You say: *"Yeah, same structure. But Rohter is more skeptical about the market size claim — let's lead with the problem and put market size in slide 3."*

Claude: *"Got it. Let's start with the problem statement. What's the one sentence that describes Rohter's specific pain?"*

You work for 90 minutes. Not managing tasks — *doing the work*, with a collaborator who keeps you on track and challenged.

---

**11:00am — You check off "Draft v1."**

The action row springs. Strikethrough wipes right. The progress bar inches forward.

Claude surfaces in the panel: *"Draft done. Based on your notes, the next action is 'Send to Scott for review.' That's a 10-minute light task — good fit for after lunch. Want me to draft the message to Scott with the doc attached?"*

You say yes. It drafts a 3-sentence message. You edit one word and send.

---

**End of week — The Retrospective.**

You close 4 outcomes this week. Waypoint surfaces:

> *Week complete. 4 outcomes closed, 1 pushed to next week (prospecting — you started it Wednesday, pattern says these take 2 weeks). Your deep work sessions averaged 94 minutes (your stated ideal is 90). 3 of 4 outcomes landed on time. One note: your email campaigns have now had 3 weeks of high open rates (43% avg) but low booking conversions (2.1% avg). Your target is 5%. Want to schedule a Focus session to rebuild the campaign strategy?*

This is the flywheel. Every closed outcome teaches the system. Every answered question makes the estimates more accurate. Every Focus session adds to a library of your best thinking. The longer you use Waypoint, the better it knows you — and the better it knows you, the more leverage it gives you.

## The Capabilities That Make This Real

### 1. The Time Intelligence Layer *(Phase 3.0)*

**What it is:** Calendar integration (Google Calendar / Outlook) that reads your day and maps your outcomes to real available time. The primary surface is an in-app **Today view** — the moment after triage where Claude proposes your committed day and you confirm it.

**The workflow:**
```
Slack brief (7:45am)  → orients you, creates pull toward the app
Triage (8:30am)       → processes new inputs, creates outcomes/actions
Today view (8:50am)   → Claude proposes the day, you commit to a plan
Outcomes / Focus      → execution against that plan
```

The Slack brief is secondary and deliberate: short, un-actionable, primes you to open the app. All planning decisions happen in the Today view, not in Slack.

**What Claude does:** Reads your open calendar windows, energy type tags on actions, and known work patterns. Proposes the committed day — which outcomes fit, in which order, with how much buffer. You adjust and confirm. Now your day has a spine, not a wishlist.

**Why it matters:** The biggest reason good intentions don't become completed work is mismatched time. You plan 6 hours of work into a 3-hour day. This closes the gap between intention and reality before you even start.

**Data model addition:**
- `calendar_events` table: external_id, title, start_at, end_at, is_blocked
- `daily_plans` table: date, committed_outcome_ids (JSON), committed_action_ids (JSON), total_estimated_minutes, confirmed_at
- Integration: OAuth to Google Calendar, pull events nightly + on morning triage

---

### 2. The Results Layer *(seeded in Phase 2.0, used from Phase 3.3)*

**What it is:** Two fields captured at archive: a required binary declaration and an optional free-text metric.

- **`outcome_result`** — `'hit'` or `'miss'`, required. You cannot archive without declaring it. This is the behavior change: it forces honest self-assessment before you close the loop.
- **`outcome_result_note`** — free text, optional. Single-line, placeholder-prompted: *"What was the actual result? e.g. '4 meetings booked', 'deck approved'"* If you leave it blank, archiving isn't blocked.

The binary gates archiving. The note captures magnitude. Both ship in Phase 2.0 — the column cost is near zero, but starting to collect result notes from day one means Phase 3.3's Pattern Memory has real data to work with rather than just hit/miss booleans.

**What Claude does:** Over time, builds a pattern model. "You set 5 meeting goals 8 times in the last 3 months. Average result: 3.2 meetings booked. You hit 5+ only when the email campaign runs before the dial session." This becomes actionable intelligence, not just history.

**Why it matters:** This is the difference between an activity log and a learning machine. Without results data, Waypoint can only track effort. With it, it can track *effectiveness*.

**Data model addition:**
- `outcome_result TEXT` — 'hit' | 'miss', required (already in Phase 2.0)
- `outcome_result_note TEXT` — optional free text (add alongside in Phase 2.0)
- Structured result parsing: Claude extracts the number/metric from free-text notes
- `outcome_patterns` view: aggregated by project + outcome type for trend surfaces

### 3. The Ambient Layer — Briefings *(Phase 3.1)*

**What it is:** Proactive push summaries. Morning brief, midday pulse, EOD wrap. Delivered via Slack DM (you already have Slack integration).

**The morning brief is secondary to the in-app Today view.** It's short and deliberately un-actionable — 4–6 lines, no decision surfaces, no buttons. Its only job is to prime you and create a pull toward the app. If you can plan your day from the brief, you don't open the app, which defeats the purpose. The brief says "here's the shape of today." The Today view is where you confirm it.

**What Claude does:** Generates a plain-language summary — active outcomes, flagged risks, available time snapshot, inbox count. Midday pulse checks trajectory against the committed plan. EOD wrap closes with wins and resets tomorrow.

**Why it matters:** Waypoint becomes part of your day even when you're not actively using it. The system develops a *relationship* with your attention, not just your workflow.

**Implementation:** Three scheduled sends (morning = 7:45am, midday = 12:00pm, EOD = 5:30pm). Each pulls from the same stats endpoints you already have. Claude generates the prose. Sent via the Slack bot you already have access to.

### 4. The Pattern Memory *(Phase 3.3)*

**What it is:** Beyond remembering how long things take — remembering *how you work*.

Examples of patterns Waypoint should learn:
- "You underestimate sales tasks by an average of 40%"
- "You complete deep work actions at an 87% rate when they're scheduled in the first 2 hours of the day"
- "Outcomes with 4+ actions have a 60% completion rate. Outcomes with ≤3 have a 90% rate."
- "You haven't done a prospecting-type outcome in 6 days (your pattern is every 3-4 days)"

**What Claude does:** Proactively surfaces these as gentle observations. Not alarm bells — more like a co-pilot noticing what the data says. "Quick note: this outcome has 7 actions. You tend to close these at a lower rate. Want me to trim it to the 3 most critical?"

**Readiness gate — this is non-negotiable:** Pattern surfacing must be gated behind sufficient data. Surfacing patterns too early with a small sample erodes trust fast — the user acts on a false pattern and stops believing the system.

- **Global floor:** No patterns surfaced until **20 closed outcomes** minimum, regardless of time elapsed. Don't use time as the gate — a power user might close 40 outcomes in 4 weeks while a normal user closes 8. Time gates punish engagement.
- **Per-pattern minimum:** Each specific pattern type requires **8 data points in that category** before it can be surfaced. (e.g., 8 sales outcomes before Claude comments on sales estimate accuracy.)
- **Confidence labeling, always:** When a pattern surfaces, Claude states the sample size. "Based on 11 email campaigns…" This lets you calibrate how much weight to give it and builds trust over time as the numbers grow.
- **Context-only surfacing:** Patterns never appear as standalone notifications or in the daily brief. They appear only when you're about to do something where the pattern is relevant — during triage, during AI breakdown, during Focus Mode. "Quick note before you estimate this: similar tasks have taken you 40% longer than planned."

**Why it matters:** This is what makes Waypoint feel like it knows you. Not in a creepy way — in a "this thing has paid attention" way. It's the difference between a smart tool and a trusted advisor.

### 5. The Second Brain — The Library *(Phase 3.2)*

**What it is:** Everything Claude helps you create in Focus Mode becomes searchable and retrievable. Its own dedicated phase — not an extension of 2.5.

Not just "Save this →" for individual messages (that's Phase 2.5, which seeds this). A *Waypoint Library* — all saved outputs, browsable and searchable, organized by outcome, tagged by type (campaign draft, pitch angle, strategy framework, etc.).

**Why it ships before Pattern Memory:** The Library feeds Phase 3.3. When Pattern Memory arrives, Claude doesn't just see session summaries (Phase 2.5) — it has a proper, tagged, searchable corpus of your best work. Ship the Library first so the data is there when the inference engine needs it.

**What Claude does:** When you open Focus Mode on a new campaign, Claude checks the Library: "You have 3 previous campaign drafts in here. The November one performed best. Want to start from that?" When you need a pitch framework, it surfaces the one you built for Henderson. Your best thinking compounds.

**Why it matters:** Most knowledge work is recreating things you've already figured out. Focus Mode sessions currently evaporate when you close them. The Library makes them permanent. You build intellectual capital over time, not just complete tasks.

---

### 6. Outcome Dependencies *(Phase 3.x)*

**What it is:** A lightweight dependency model. Some outcomes need other outcomes to be done first.

You already have `blocked_by` on actions. The vision is to surface this at the outcome level: "This outcome is blocking 2 others. It's your highest-leverage item today."

**What Claude does:** When you're in triage or planning your day, Claude flags the critical path. "You can't send to Rohter until Scott approves. Scott can't approve until the deck is drafted. The deck hasn't been started. That's the chain — want me to put 'Draft v1' at the top of today's plan?"

**Why it matters:** Working at the outcome level means you need to see relationships between outcomes, not just tasks within them. This is the difference between busyness and leverage.

---

### 7. Capture Everywhere *(Phase 3.x)*

**What it is:** Multiple input vectors beyond Slack.

Right now: Slack → inbox → triage. Future:
- **Slack Bot:** `/waypoint [text]` creates a triage item instantly from any Slack channel or DM
- **Email Forward:** Forward any email to your Waypoint address → auto-triaged and waiting in inbox
- **Voice Note in Focus Mode:** "Claude, note that the campaign needs a stronger CTA" → saved to session notes
- **Web Clipper** (stretch): browser extension to clip content as a research action under an outcome

**Why it matters:** Commitments and ideas don't only arrive via Slack. They come in email, in conversations, mid-meeting. The app that captures everything wins.

---

### 8. Stakeholder Visibility *(Phase 4.x — carefully)*

**What it is:** Read-only shareable "status cards" for individual outcomes.

Not collaboration — Waypoint stays single-user. But sometimes an outcome involves someone else, and that person wants to know where things stand. A shareable link that shows: outcome title, actions done/total, deadline, last updated. That's it. Read-only. No login required.

**What Claude does:** When you archive an outcome, Claude optionally drafts an "outcome summary" — 2 sentences on what was accomplished, for forwarding to stakeholders. No digging through Slack threads.

**Why it matters:** A huge amount of professional friction is status updates. "Where are we on X?" If an outcome has a living status card, you never have to write that Slack message.

## The AI Layer — What Claude Actually Becomes

Here's the thing about every major capability above: **Claude is the connective tissue**.

Claude isn't a feature in Waypoint. Claude is what makes every piece of Waypoint intelligent. The progression:

**Today (Phase 1.x):** Claude organizes. It groups inbox items, breaks outcomes into actions, reprioritizes. It acts on your behalf when you invoke it.

**Phase 2.x:** Claude assists. It's inside Focus Mode with you, asking questions, drafting, remembering. It learns your patterns and corrects for your blind spots.

**Phase 3.x:** Claude plans. It sees your calendar, your outcomes, your available time, and builds you a day. It knows your patterns well enough to say "you're overcommitting again" before you do.

**Phase 4.x:** Claude advises. It's looked at 6 months of your outcomes, your results, your Focus sessions. It has a perspective on what's working and what isn't. It proactively surfaces the observations that change how you work.

The model isn't "Claude as a chatbot you query." It's Claude as a system that's *always running in the background*, accumulating context, and surfacing it at exactly the right moment.

## The Flywheel

This is the most important section.

Every tool starts useful. Very few tools get *more* useful over time. Waypoint has the architecture to be the exception — but only if the feedback loop is designed explicitly.

**The loop:**

```
You close an outcome
  → Result captured (did it work?)
  → Reflection stored (what would you do differently?)
  → Session notes saved (what did Claude help you build?)
  → Pattern updated (how long did it actually take vs estimate?)

Next similar outcome surfaces
  → Context injected (past results, saved outputs, time corrections)
  → Claude says "last time this took 3 hours, not 90 mins — want me to update the estimate?"
  → Focus session opens with relevant past work preloaded
  → Draft starts from your best previous version

Close the outcome faster, with better output, with less friction
  → Repeat
```

This is the compounding effect. Month 1: Waypoint is a smarter to-do list. Month 3: Waypoint knows your patterns. Month 6: Waypoint makes you measurably more effective at the specific type of work you do. Month 12: You cannot imagine how you worked before it.

**The quantification:** One of the things that will make Waypoint sticky for the long term is showing you the proof. Not vanity metrics — real ones:

- **Estimate accuracy over time** (are your time estimates getting more accurate as context builds?)
- **Completion rate by outcome type** (where are you consistently strong vs. weak?)
- **Time to first action after triage** (is the morning ritual getting faster?)
- **Results rate** (what percentage of your outcomes achieve their stated result?)

These are metrics that prove the product is working. Build them. Show them. Make them undeniable.

## What To Build Next (And What To Not Build)

### Do these, in this order:

1. **Finish Phase 2.0–2.6 as scoped.** They're right. The foundation needs to be right before you build higher.
2. **The Results fields on archive** — both fields ship in Phase 2.0: `outcome_result` (binary, required, gates archive) and `outcome_result_note` (free text, optional). The column cost is near zero. The data you start collecting now feeds Phase 3.3. Don't wait.
3. **Phase 3.0 — Calendar Integration + Today view.** The in-app Today view is the primary committed-day surface. Calendar integration (Google/Outlook OAuth) makes it real. This is the biggest unlock after 2.x.
4. **Phase 3.1 — Morning Brief via Slack.** Small build, massive daily-driver impact. Keep it short, un-actionable, and pointed at opening the app. Builds the habit anchor.
5. **Phase 3.2 — The Library.** Searchable, tagged archive of Focus Mode outputs. Ships before Pattern Memory because it feeds it.
6. **Phase 3.3 — Pattern Memory + Execution Analytics.** Build this once you have 20+ closed outcomes and months of result notes. Gate it correctly (see capability section) or it erodes trust instead of building it.

### Don't build these yet:

- **Multi-user collaboration.** Waypoint's power comes from being *yours*. Adding collaboration changes the product fundamentally and requires a different architecture. Table it until the single-user experience is undeniably excellent.
- **Mobile-native app.** The mobile web version (Phase 1.5/1.6) is the right call. Native app is a massive investment — wait until the product is proven and daily-driver lock-in is established.
- **Integrations with every tool** (Asana, Linear, Jira, HubSpot, etc.). Don't become an aggregator. Stay opinionated. Slack is the right input pipe because Slack is where work-as-language happens. Add integrations only where there's a clear, specific outcome-level need.

## The Product Identity

Here is the one-liner that everything should be in service of:

> **Waypoint is the place where intention becomes execution, and execution becomes learning.**

Not a task manager. Not a project tracker. Not a Notion clone. An execution OS that gets smarter about *you* every time you use it — and that closes the loop from idea to result to pattern to better work.

That's the product. Everything in the roadmap is either getting you closer to that or it isn't. Use it as a filter.
